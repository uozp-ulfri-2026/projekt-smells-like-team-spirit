# Problem statements this project can solve

This project is not just a map of articles. It helps answer a broader question:

> **How do news topics move across geography and time, and where is attention concentrated or missing?**

Below are concrete problems the application can address.

## 1. Seeing what the newsroom covers, not just what it publishes
A large archive of articles is difficult to browse manually. Readers and analysts often want to know:

- Which countries appear most often in the news?
- Which regions are only mentioned in bursts?
- Are some parts of the world consistently over- or under-covered?

The app makes this visible through geographic aggregation and time filtering.

## 2. Understanding how coverage changes over time
News coverage is dynamic. A topic may appear briefly, peak during a crisis, and disappear again.

This project can help answer:

- When did a topic first become prominent?
- How long did it stay in the news?
- Did attention spread from one region to another?

The timeline slider and playback-style exploration are well suited for this kind of analysis.

## 3. Finding the geographic footprint of a topic
Many topics are not purely local or national. They spread across multiple countries and cities.

Examples of questions the app can support:

- Where are political, economic, or sports stories concentrated?
- Which countries are linked to a particular theme?
- Is a topic mostly local, regional, or international?

This is useful for spotting clusters that would be hard to see from article lists alone.

## 4. Detecting blind spots in coverage
Every newsroom has editorial patterns. Some regions get a lot of attention, others very little.

The app can support a problem like:

- Are there countries or cities that appear only in exceptional events?
- Which themes dominate attention in a given region?
- What is not being covered, even when the data suggests it should be?

That makes the project useful for editorial reflection, not just browsing.

## 5. Quickly comparing countries and themes
When looking at thousands of articles, it is hard to compare coverage by eye.

The app helps compare:

- Country vs. country
- Topic vs. topic
- A short time window vs. a longer period

This can be turned into a strong presentation story: not just "what happened", but "what gets attention where".

## 6. Supporting topic-driven news discovery
Sometimes a user knows the theme, but not the relevant articles.

For example:

- A journalist wants to find all articles about a country-related event.
- A student wants to inspect how a specific topic evolved.
- A researcher wants to browse examples before doing deeper analysis.

The interactive list, filters, and map make this a practical discovery tool.

## 7. Identifying event hotspots and spreading narratives
News often starts in one place and then expands into broader coverage.

The project can help show:

- Where an event originated
- Which places are repeatedly associated with it
- Whether coverage is tied to a single location or a wider network of places

That makes it useful for event-oriented analysis, not only for static dashboards.

## 8. Turning a large archive into an explainable story
A raw article archive is too big for a short presentation. The app turns it into an explainable visual narrative.

It can answer questions such as:

- What does this archive look like at a glance?
- Which geographic patterns stand out immediately?
- What does the data suggest about editorial focus?

This is ideal for a 3-minute demo, because the value is visible immediately.

## 9. A practical angle for journalism and media studies
The project can be framed as a tool for:

- journalists who want fast situational awareness,
- media researchers studying coverage patterns,
- students who need an interpretable archive view,
- anyone trying to understand how public attention is distributed geographically.

## 10. The short version for the presentation
If you need one sentence for the pitch, use this:

**Our application helps users explore how news coverage is distributed across countries and over time, so they can spot patterns, gaps, and shifts in attention in a large article archive.**
